# The-Influence-of-Supreme-Court-Decisions-on-Public-Opinion-Shifts
This project examines how major Supreme Court decisions shape public opinion in the United States, focusing on abortion rights and two landmark cases, Roe v. Wade in 1973 and Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization in 2022. The goal is to understand how judicial rulings influence public attitudes over time and how those attitudes shift across political, demographic, and regional groups.

The project looks at the relationship between the Supreme Court and public opinion as something that works both ways. Court decisions do not just reflect society, they can also push change, create backlash, and reshape political debates. By comparing Roe and Dobbs, this research highlights how decisions made decades apart can produce very different reactions depending on the political and social context.

The analysis combines survey data with media analysis to get a full picture of opinion change. Public opinion data from sources like Pew Research Center, Gallup, and the General Social Survey is used to track long term trends, while media coverage is analyzed to understand how framing influences how people interpret these decisions. This mixed approach allows the project to connect changes in opinion with both legal decisions and the way those decisions are presented to the public.

Key findings from the project include:

Supreme Court decisions lead to noticeable shifts in public opinion, especially on highly controversial issues like abortion
The Dobbs decision caused a sharp decline in trust and favorability toward the Supreme Court
Public opinion became more polarized after Dobbs, with clear differences across political parties and regions
Media framing plays a major role in shaping how people understand and react to Court decisions
Demographic factors such as age, gender, and political affiliation influence how individuals respond to these rulings

The results show that Roe led to gradual changes in public opinion over time, while Dobbs created a more immediate and intense reaction. In particular, Dobbs increased political polarization and contributed to a decline in trust in the Supreme Court, especially among groups that supported abortion rights. The findings also suggest that when long standing rights are reversed, the public response tends to be stronger than when new rights are introduced.

This project contributes to a broader understanding of how law and public opinion interact in American politics. It shows that the Supreme Court is not just a legal institution, but also a political and social force that can shape national debates and influence how people think about rights and government authority.

This repository is useful for students, researchers, and anyone interested in constitutional law, public opinion, and the role of the Supreme Court in shaping social and political change in the United States.
